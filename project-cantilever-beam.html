A Deflection-Based Scale to Measure Weight

Euler–Bernoulli Theory MATLAB Fabrication Brown Design Workshop

A working mechanical scale that reads an object's weight from how far a cantilever beam bends — designed, built, and calibrated from first principles using Euler–Bernoulli beam theory and Hooke's law.

At a glance

The goal: predict an unknown mass from beam deflection alone. Because a cantilever bends linearly with load, deflection maps directly to weight through the beam's spring constant. I derived the governing equations, sized the beam in MATLAB for a safe stress margin, fabricated it in the Brown Design Workshop, and calibrated it against known masses.

The design

The scale is a cantilever: a 0.96 m acrylic bar (38.1 × 9.525 mm cross-section) fixed at one end to a wooden upright bolted to a plywood base. A second wood block sandwiches and screws the acrylic to the upright so the fixed end stays rigid. Loads hang from a small platform glued at L/6 — 0.16 m from the support — chosen to keep the beam well within its elastic limit while still giving a readable deflection.

The analysis

Modeling the bar with Euler–Bernoulli beam theory and a point load at L/6 (neglecting beam self-weight), I solved the deflection equation piecewise across the load to get closed-form expressions for the tip deflection and the spring constant:

δ = 2PL³ / 81EI   ·   k = P/δ = 81EI / 2L³

With E = 3 GPa for acrylic and a rectangular second moment of area I = 2.74 × 10⁻⁹ m⁴, the predicted spring constant came out to k = 376.79 N/m. Checking stresses against the 60 MPa yield strength gave safety factors from 22 at 1 kg down to 3.7 at the 6 kg maximum — comfortably safe across the whole range.

Process

1

Derive the model

Set up the cantilever with a load at L/6, applied the boundary conditions (zero deflection and slope at the wall, matched slope across the load), and integrated to closed-form deflection and spring-constant expressions.

2

Size it in MATLAB

Swept beam dimensions to compute I, k, bending stress, deflection, and safety factor — choosing a geometry that maximizes readable deflection while keeping the safety factor well above 1 across 1–6 kg.

3

Fabricate

Cut and assembled the acrylic beam, wooden upright, and plywood base in the Brown Design Workshop, screwing the support block to fix the beam end.

4

Calibrate

Loaded known masses from 1–6 kg and measured tip deflection against a reference bar, marking the deflection bands directly on the stand.

5

Regress & validate

Fit lines through both datasets in MATLAB to extract the experimental spring constant, then predicted two unknown masses to test the finished scale.

Calibration

To read deflection, I placed a reference bar perpendicular to the base and measured where the acrylic beam rested under each load. The deflection bands for 1–6 kg were marked straight onto the upright.

Results

Linear regression on the calibration data gave an experimental spring constant of k = 395.88 N/m — within 5% of the theoretical 376.79 N/m. Measured and predicted deflection tracked closely across the whole range.

Using the calibrated scale, the team predicted two unknown masses at 3.7 kg and 5.5 kg — within 9% and 6% of their true values. Both fell inside the 10% target, so the design was deemed accurate. The small remaining error traces mostly to neglecting the beam's self-weight and slight forward bending of the wooden support under load.

MATLAB (core of the model)

The script computes the second moment of area, spring constant, bending stress, safety factor, and deflection, then runs the regression for the experimental k.

% beam + acrylic bar dimensions width = 0.0381; % m (1.5 in) thickness= 0.009525; % m (0.375 in) length = 0.96; % m I = (width*thickness^3)/12; % second moment of area E = 3e9; % Pa, acrylic g = 9.81; % spring constant for load at L/6 k = (81*E*I) / (2*length^3); % ≈ 376.79 N/m % stress + safety factor at the extremes c = thickness/2; sigma_y = 60e6; % Pa, yield % ... bending moment M = P*L/6, sigma = M*c/I, S = sigma_y/sigma % experimental spring constant via linear regression x = [0;1;2;3;4;5;6]; y2 = [0;2.5;4.75;7.5;9.75;12.40;15]; % measured Δ (cm) b2 = x\y2; k_experimental = 1 / ((b2/100)/9.81); % ≈ 395.88 N/m

Full script (stress, deflection, and plotting) is in the project report.

What I took away

This was theory made tangible: a hand-derived deflection equation that actually predicted real masses to within a few percent. It also taught me where idealized models break — self-weight and an imperfectly rigid support both nudged the results — and why calibration matters as much as derivation.
