Next-Gen Material Selection — Vertebral Body Replacement

Ansys Granta Material Selection SolidWorks FEA Implant Design

A case study that searches the entire engineering-materials universe — 4,395 materials — for a next-generation polymer to replace titanium in spinal implants, reducing stress shielding by mechanically matching bone. The recommendation: polyetherimide (PEI) — which I then modeled and stress-tested in SolidWorks.

View full poster (PDF) ↗

At a glance

Titanium is a current standard for vertebral body replacements, but it is far stiffer than bone — so it carries load the surrounding vertebrae should carry, causing them to weaken over time (stress shielding). PEEK matches bone better but integrates poorly with it. Using Ansys Granta EduPack, I built a multi-stage screening pipeline to find a material that balances bone-like stiffness, strength, toughness, durability, and biocompatibility — then validated the winner with a finite-element study in SolidWorks.

The problem: stress shielding

Osteoporosis, spinal tumors, and infections can compromise the mechanical stability of the spine, and a vertebral body replacement restores it after the diseased vertebra is resected. The implant must bear high physiological compressive loads and integrate with surrounding bone for the long term. Titanium is biocompatible and corrosion-resistant, but its stiffness (~110 GPa) dwarfs that of cortical bone (3–20 GPa). When an implant is far stiffer than the bone around it, it absorbs most of the load — and unloaded bone resorbs and weakens. PEEK sits much closer to bone mechanically and is radiolucent, but it integrates poorly with bone. The goal: a material stiff enough to be safe, compliant enough to share load, and biologically suited to long-term implantation.

Screening method

Starting from the full "All materials" universe in Ansys Granta EduPack (Bioengineering Level 3), I applied a sequence of quantitative limit stages, then qualitative refinement stages, progressively narrowing 4,395 materials down to a short list of finalists.

Result: polyetherimide (PEI)

Eight materials survived every stage: epoxy SMC and polyester/E-glass composites, PAI, PEEK, PEI, PI, and two PPS variants. PEEK was set aside as already in clinical use. Epoxy (hydrolysis-prone, cytotoxic) and polyester/E-glass (borderline brittle at 2% elongation) were ruled out, and pure PPS suffers low stiffness and osteoconductivity. That left PI, PAI, and PEI — all promising, but PI and PAI lack FDA orthopedic approval. PEI won out: bone-like stiffness, compressive strength within PEEK's range, demonstrated in-vitro biocompatibility, the highest fatigue strength of the finalists, a lower price than PAI and PI, and — uniquely — existing FDA approval for external bone-fixation systems.

FEA validation in SolidWorks

To test the recommendation as an actual device, I modeled a simplified vertebral-body cage (a Ø 14 mm hollow cylinder) sandwiched between two cortical-bone segments, then ran an identical static compression study (1,000 N physiological lumbar load, bottom face fixed) twice — once with a titanium implant and once with PEI — changing nothing but the implant material. The question: how much load reaches the surrounding bone in each case?

Results

MetricTitanium implantPEI implant
Peak von Mises stress in bone12.54 MPa27.04 MPa
Avg. bone stress near interface12.10 MPa24.49 MPa
Peak stress in implant23.69 MPa13.39 MPa (< 105 MPa yield ✓)

As expected, the titanium implant absorbs most of the compressive load, leaving the surrounding bone understressed — the mechanical signature of stress shielding. The PEI implant, with stiffness much closer to bone, transfers more load into the bone, which should help preserve bone density over time. The peak stress in the PEI implant itself stays comfortably below its yield strength, confirming it survives physiological loading.

A simplified comparative study to illustrate the stress-shielding mechanism, not a validated clinical model. Simplifications: idealized cylindrical geometry, bonded contact, homogeneous isotropic bone, and a single static load.

Where this goes next

Future work would move toward realism: patient-specific cage geometry with porosity, frictional contact at the bone interface, anisotropic bone properties, and cyclic fatigue loading. On the materials side, PEI's long-term in-vivo wear, degradation, and osseointegration — potentially aided by bioactive coatings or fiber reinforcement — remain open questions worth investigating.
